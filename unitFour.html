Monetary Base: Frequency [Monthly]

The monetary base includes currency in circulation and bank reserves held at the Fed. It's the foundation of money creation. This graph is essential for illustrating how central bank actions like quantitative easing increase the base — which commercial banks can then multiply into broader money supply.

M2 Money Stock: Frequency [Monthly]

M2 is a broad measure of the money supply, including cash, checking deposits, and easily convertible near-money like savings deposits. Tracking M2 helps show how the Federal Reserve’s actions influence liquidity in the economy. A rapid rise may signal expansionary policy or inflationary pressure.

Interest on Reserve Balances: Frequency [Daily]

IORB is the interest rate the Fed pays on reserves held by banks. It acts as a floor for short-term interest rates and is a newer tool used in post-2008 monetary policy. Tracking this rate helps students understand how the Fed controls bank incentives and overall liquidity.

Bank Credit Commercial Banks: Frequency [Weekly]

Bank credit reflects the total loans and leases issued by commercial banks. It helps visualize how banks expand the money supply through lending. A sharp rise suggests credit expansion, while declines may signal tighter credit conditions or reduced demand for loans.

Velocity of M2 Money Stock: Frequency [Quarterly]

The velocity of money measures how frequently a dollar is spent in the economy. A falling M2 velocity implies that even with more money in circulation, economic activity may remain sluggish. It’s essential for understanding why inflation doesn’t always rise with monetary expansion.

Effective Federal Funds Rate: Frequency [Daily]

This is the interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans and serves as the Federal Reserve’s primary policy tool. Changes in this rate influence borrowing costs, investment, and ultimately aggregate demand. It's key to visualizing the stance of monetary policy over time.
